The Land and Housing Survey in a Global Sample of Cities is part of a broader research initiative—titled Monitoring Global Urban Expansion — undertaken by the NYU Stern Urbanization Project in partnership with the United Nations Human Settlements Programme (UN-Habitat) and the Lincoln Institute of Land Policy. The main objective of this initiative is to add a spatial dimension to monitoring global urbanization, and to support evidence-based planning and policy making in cities and metropolitan areas the world over.

The Land and Housing Survey in a Global Sample of Cities includes: 1) Survey of the Regulatory Regime Governing Land and Housing, which captures land ownership patterns, land-use planning, and development of new subdivisions in expansion areas of the city and 2) The Affordability Survey, which compiles costs of different types of land, houses, and apartments available for sale or rent in the city.

The global sample of cities includes 200 cities of different size from all world regions, 5% of the 4,000+ cities and metropolitan areas that had 100,000 people or more in 2010. The results of the study will be presented at Habitat III, the United Nations Conference on Housing and Sustainable Urban Development, now scheduled to take place in Quito, Ecuador in October 2016.

FREQUENTLY ASKED QUESTIONS
 
 

What is the purpose of the surveys?

The purpose of the surveys is to monitor urban expansion and look more specifically at the rules and regulations governing the development of new residential land and housing, their enforcement and the affordability and access to jobs of housing units and residential plots.

How do the surveys inform the Urban Expansion Program and produce knowledge on urbanization and cities?

The surveys represent integral parts of the Urban Expansion Program. The other phases of our global monitoring effort -- the new Atlas of Urban Expansion, the Quality of Urban Layouts in Expansion Areas and the Population and Urban Extent Projections for all 4,000+ Cities, 2000-2040-- complete this larger effort to monitor and present solid empirical data on actual urban expansion and its key attributes in cities around the world over long periods of time. This data can demonstrate the extent, the form, and the context of global urban expansion, and suggest how and by how much they are likely to grow further in the future.

How will the results of the surveys inform city officials, planners and policy makers?

The surveys are part of a larger effort to monitor the state of the world’s cities in a more scientific manner, so as to enable municipal and national governments, as well as civic groups and international organizations, to engage more effectively in evidence-based planning and policy-making especially when it comes to urban expansion.

How will the surveys be conducted?

The surveys will be completed by city-based researchers with knowledge on each of the cities in the sample. The city-based researchers will be supported by regional coordinators and survey leaders, based in New York. The project will be conducted under the supervision and guidance of Shlomo Angel, Senior Research Scholar and leader of the NYU Urban Expansion Program.

Why was my city selected?

Your city is part of the stratified global sample of 200 cities in our sample. Our sample is stratified into three strata: eight world regions, four city population size categories, and three groups of countries with different number of cities. In general, the sample is drawn with the urban population in mind: it seeks to focus on urban dwellers rather than on cities. The sample thus contains 5% of the cities in the universe and 27% of the urban population in the universe. We have conducted a statistical test to determine whether the sample is truly representative of the universe of cities by checking whether the mean population growth rate in the sample is the same as that of the universe as a whole. We can say with 95% confidence that the two are not different from each other.

Will my answers remain confidential?

Your answers to the survey will be confidential and the survey team guaranties full confidentiality. That said, if you would like to be acknowledged for your participation in the survey, we can list your name as one of the contributors.

 
 
 
What do we do?
 
 
Monitoring Global Expansion.
 
 

Monitoring Global Urban Expansion seeks to understand both the quantitative and the qualitative attributes and characteristics of urban expansion in the entire Universe of Cities, defined as all the 4,000+ cities and metropolitan areas that had more than 100,000 inhabitants in 2010. At this stage, we focus on a scientifically constructed global sample of 200 cities, consisting of roughly 5% of the universe of cities, and containing approximately one-quarter of the world urban population. The sample is stratified, representing cities in four city-size categories in eight world regions. Currently, the initiative consists of three phases.

Phase I: The Atlas of Urban Expansion — The 2015 Edition

The Atlas provides satellite-based maps of urban extent and their associated metrics— including built-up area, built-up area density, fragmentation, and compactness—for a global sample of 200 cities for the years 1990, 2000 and 2013.

Phase II: The Quality of the Urban Fabric in Expansion Areas

The quality of the urban fabric in expansion areas is determined through an intensive mapping effort that measures (1) the share of land in arterial roads and access to arterial roads; (2) the share of land in streets and the distribution of street widths; (3) the share of land in residential areas in different stages of the housing sector evolution (atomistic housing, informal land subdivisions, formal land subdivisions, and housing projects); (4) average block size and the density of street intersections; and (5) plot dimensions in land subdivisions.

Phase III: The Land and Housing Survey in the Global Sample of Cities

The Land and Housing Survey in the Global Sample of Cities has two objectives. The first objective of the survey is to obtain information on the regulatory regime governing the development of residential land and housing on the urban fringe, a regime than can be detrimental in ensuring that residential land remains plentiful, affordable and accessible and that development on the urban fringe is orderly, so as to facilitate the provision of public works as well as public transport. The second objective of the survey is to ascertain whether residential land and housing in the city are indeed plentiful, affordable, and accessible.

Shlomo (Solly) Angel

Adjunct Professor and Senior Research Scholar

http://sollyangel.com

Shlomo (Solly) Angel is an adjunct professor at NYU and senior research scholar at the NYU Stern Urbanization Project, where he leads the Urban Expansion initiative. Angel is an expert on urban development policy, having advised the United Nations, the World Bank, and the Inter-American Development Bank (IDB). He currently focuses on documenting and planning for urban expansion in the developing world.

In 1973, he started a program in Human Settlements Planning and Development at the Asian Institute of Technology in Bangkok. He taught at the Institute from 1973 to 1983, while researching housing and urban development in the cities of East, South, and Southeast Asia. From the mid-80s to mid-90s, he worked as a housing and urban development consultant to UN-Habitat, the Asian Development Bank, and the Government of Thailand. In 2000, he published Housing Policy Matters, a comparative study of housing conditions and policies around the world. From 2000 onward, he prepared housing sector assessments of 11 Latin America and Caribbean countries for the IDB and the World Bank.

Angel earned a bachelor’s degree in architecture and a doctorate in city and regional planning at the University of California, Berkeley.
